Communication – Basics & Process

Communication is the process of exchanging information, ideas, thoughts, or feelings between two or more people through a common system of symbols, signs, or behaviour.

Elements of the Communication Process

ElementDescription
SenderThe person who initiates the message.
EncodingConverting thoughts into words/symbols.
MessageThe actual information being conveyed.
Channel / MediumThe path through which the message travels (speech, email, letter, etc.).
DecodingReceiver interpreting the message.
ReceiverThe person who receives and understands the message.
FeedbackThe response sent back to the sender.
NoiseAny disturbance that distorts the message.
ContextThe setting/environment of the communication.

7 Cs of Effective Communication

  • Clear – Use simple, precise language.
  • Concise – Avoid unnecessary words.
  • Concrete – Use specific facts and figures.
  • Correct – Grammar, facts and tone must be accurate.
  • Coherent – Logical flow of ideas.
  • Complete – All necessary information is included.
  • Courteous – Respectful, polite and considerate.
✅ Exam Tip: Questions on the 7 Cs are very common. Learn one short example for each C.

Types of Communication

A. Based on Mode

TypeDefinitionExamples
VerbalUsing spoken or written words.Speeches, letters, phone calls
Non-verbalWithout words — body language, gestures.Nodding, eye contact, posture
VisualUsing images, charts, videos.Graphs, presentations, signs

B. Based on Direction (Organisational)

  • Downward – From superior to subordinate (instructions, orders).
  • Upward – From subordinate to superior (reports, feedback).
  • Horizontal / Lateral – Between peers at the same level.
  • Diagonal – Across departments and levels.

C. Formal vs Informal

FormalInformal
StructurePre-defined channelsNo fixed channel
LanguageOfficial, professionalCasual, colloquial
ExampleOfficial memo, business emailConversation, social chat
SpeedSlower (official process)Faster
ReliabilityHigh, documentedMay be distorted (grapevine)

Non-verbal Communication – Body Language (KINESICS)

  • Gestures – Hand and arm movements.
  • Facial expressions – Smile, frown, raised eyebrows.
  • Eye contact – Shows attention and confidence.
  • Posture – Open vs closed body positions.
  • Proxemics – Use of personal space (intimate, personal, social, public).
  • Paralanguage – Tone, pitch, pace, volume of voice.

Barriers to Communication

Barriers are factors that prevent the effective exchange of ideas or information.

Types of Barriers

BarrierCauseHow to Overcome
Physical / Environmental Noise, distance, poor technology Use appropriate channel; reduce noise
Linguistic / Semantic Different language, jargon, ambiguity Use simple, clear language; define terms
Psychological / Emotional Fear, stress, prejudice, poor listening Empathy, active listening, open mind
Cultural Different customs, values, interpretations Cultural awareness and sensitivity
Organisational Complex hierarchy, information overload Clear policies, flatten hierarchy
Perceptual Different viewpoints, stereotyping Active listening, avoid assumptions
Technological Poor connectivity, outdated equipment Upgrade tools, test before use
📌 Remember: Always give one example per barrier type in exam answers.

Listening Skills

Listening is an active process of receiving, constructing meaning from, and responding to spoken messages.

Types of Listening

  • Active Listening – Full concentration, understanding, responding.
  • Passive Listening – Hearing without much engagement (e.g., background music).
  • Critical Listening – Evaluating and analysing what is heard.
  • Empathetic Listening – Understanding the speaker's feelings.
  • Discriminative Listening – Distinguishing different sounds or tones.
  • Informational Listening – Listening to learn (lectures, instructions).

Barriers to Listening

  • External distractions / noise
  • Pre-judgement and bias
  • Information overload
  • Emotional state
  • Physical tiredness

Active Listening Skills (LADDER)

  • Look at the speaker
  • Ask questions
  • Don't interrupt
  • Don't change the subject
  • Empathise
  • Respond appropriately

Reading Skills

Types of Reading

TypePurpose
SkimmingReading quickly for the gist / main idea.
ScanningLooking for specific information (dates, names).
Intensive ReadingClose, careful reading for full comprehension.
Extensive ReadingReading large amounts for pleasure/general understanding.
Critical ReadingAnalysing and evaluating arguments in a text.

Comprehension Techniques (SQ3R)

  • Survey – Get an overview.
  • Question – Ask questions before reading.
  • Read – Read carefully.
  • Recite – Summarise in own words.
  • Review – Go back and check understanding.
✅ Exam Tip: For comprehension questions — always read the questions first, then skim the passage to locate answers.

Writing Skills – Letters & Emails

Business Letter – Parts

  1. Sender's Address
  2. Date
  3. Receiver's Address (Inside Address)
  4. Subject Line
  5. Salutation (Dear Sir/Madam)
  6. Body (Opening ▸ Main Content ▸ Closing)
  7. Complimentary Close (Yours faithfully / sincerely)
  8. Signature & Designation
  9. Enclosures (if any)

Types of Business Letters

  • Enquiry Letter – Requesting information.
  • Complaint Letter – Reporting a problem.
  • Adjustment Letter – Responding to a complaint.
  • Order Letter – Placing an order.
  • Sales Letter – Promoting a product.
  • Job Application Letter – Applying for a position.
  • Cover Letter – Accompanying a résumé/CV.

Professional Email – Guidelines

  • Use a clear, specific Subject Line.
  • Begin with a proper Salutation.
  • Keep the body brief and focused.
  • Use formal language; avoid slang or abbreviations.
  • End with a polite closing (Regards / Best regards).
  • Include your full email signature.
  • Proofread before sending.

Memo (Memorandum)

A memo is an internal written communication used within an organisation.

  • TO: Recipient(s)
  • FROM: Sender
  • DATE:
  • SUBJECT:
  • Body – concise, direct

Report Writing

A Report is a formal document that presents facts, findings, and recommendations on a specific topic.

Types of Reports

  • Informational Report – Presents facts without analysis.
  • Analytical Report – Includes analysis, conclusions, and recommendations.
  • Technical Report – For engineering/scientific purposes.
  • Progress Report – Status of an ongoing project.
  • Feasibility Report – Assesses viability of a project.

Structure of a Formal Report

  1. Title Page
  2. Table of Contents
  3. Abstract / Executive Summary
  4. Introduction (Purpose, Scope, Background)
  5. Methodology
  6. Findings / Results
  7. Discussion / Analysis
  8. Conclusion
  9. Recommendations
  10. References / Bibliography
  11. Appendices
📌 Key Difference: Abstract = summary of the whole report. Introduction = background and purpose only.

Presentation Skills

Stages of a Presentation

StageWhat to Do
IntroductionGreet, introduce yourself, state topic and objectives.
BodyPresent main points clearly with transitions.
ConclusionSummarise, give final message, invite questions.

Effective Presentation Tips

  • Know your audience and purpose.
  • Use the Rule of Three – limit to 3 main points.
  • Maintain eye contact with the audience.
  • Use clear visuals (not too much text on slides).
  • Vary your tone and pace; avoid monotone delivery.
  • Handle questions confidently; say "Good question" to buy time.
  • Practice the KISS principle – Keep It Short and Simple.

Slide Design Best Practices

  • Max 6–7 lines per slide; max 7 words per line.
  • Use high-contrast colours and readable fonts.
  • One idea per slide.
  • Use graphs/images instead of dense text.

Group Discussion & Interviews

Group Discussion (GD)

A GD is a structured discussion among a group of candidates assessed by evaluators.

Do's in a GD

  • Initiate if you have good knowledge of the topic.
  • Speak clearly and confidently.
  • Listen actively and build on others' points.
  • Maintain positive body language.
  • Summarise at the end if asked.

Don'ts in a GD

  • Don't interrupt or talk over others.
  • Don't monopolise the discussion.
  • Don't use aggressive or dismissive language.
  • Don't go off-topic.

✅ Interview Etiquette: Dress formally, arrive early, carry extra copies of your CV, maintain eye contact, and send a thank-you note afterwards.
